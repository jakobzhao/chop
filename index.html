Welcome！

This map reconstructs the Capital Hill Organization Protest (CHOP) with a unique drone image collected during the protest in June 2020. As Seattle's law enforcement sweeps the CHOP, this digital geographical archive still provides a way to engage with this social movement through the geovisualization of graffiti, encampments, barriers, and more. On this map, you can:

  • View an interactive map of the CHOP, the graffiti, and people's memories;
  • Share your in-situ experiences onto this map; and
  • Download the drone image of the CHOP for your projects.

We welcome everyone's contribution. If you have any ideas to improve this web map, please contact us about any questions you have.

Statement

Like many others in 2020, I had watched year after year of police killings of people of color in the US. When I watched the video of the killing of George Floyd, I felt physically sick. I’m not sure whether it was the build up of so many unjustified killings, or something about the circumstances or brutality of this particular man’s death that broke through something for me and many around the world. Why it took this extreme level of violence and disregard for human life to take action is a subject of ongoing introspection for some.

The day of Floyd’s death, I saw a helicopter hovering over Seattle and wondered what was happening. It came the next day, and the day after that. It was a Police helicopter, monitoring the beginnings of the George Floyd protests.

People flocked to Downtown Seattle during those days to assemble and march throughout the city streets. There was sadness, anger, and rage in the faces of the people. Some had taken to the streets before, trying to bring attention to the causes of social justice - organizing, advocating, resisting. For others, it was visibly their first time participating in a demonstration - confronting a line of police officers in riot gear, confronting the names of people of color killed by police scrawled on cardboard signs, confronting their own path in life and prejudices.

Each day brought more people to the downtown. People recognized people from the day before. A notion of community emerged.

In those first few days, the Seattle Police Department tried to control the crowds with teargas; Their blindness to the moment and predispositions confirmed the moral authority and mission of those that had taken the streets. The Seattle Police Department’s East Precinct, located squarely in Capitol Hill’s art district, was literally and figuratively overrun by protesters.

Video after video of police officers killing unarmed black men had compromised the public’s trust in the Police nationwide and violated their contract to exercise their power to enforce law and order. Demonstrators drove the police out of their immediate public space, and re-incorporated it as a self-asserted independent geography called “Capitol Hill Autonomous Zone (CHAZ)” or “Capitol Hill Organized Protest (CHOP).”

If the group could remove something from the community, it found it could add things too - food, water, camping spaces, a vegetable garden - even “informal” social services such as a community watch and medical tent. The events of CHAZ/CHOP produced emotional re-interpretations and physical re-configurations of space that (temporarily) evolved a novel urban space.

There was no singular reason that motivated me to capture imagery of CHOP/CHAZ. In fact, it was the opposite - there was such an overwhelming feeling of discrete threads and intersecting stories that it felt like capturing it in this way might be one of the only ways to get a full sense for everything that it was. I wanted to have a public record that people who want more can access, relate, and react to for themselves.

It’s my hope that people who access the imagery might find some deeper personal understanding about the events that preceded it, be inspired by the creation that took place in and around CHAZ/CHOP, and witness a glimpse of what one group of people did when the actions of their government violated their values (Even if the viewer may disagree with their political basis.)
